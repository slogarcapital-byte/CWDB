# CWDB Unit Economics (Phase 1 targets)

**Revenue model:** Pay-per-accepted-bid. Contractor pays $1,000 when they win a deck job sourced from one of our leads.

**Why this model matters:** It's the only pricing model we've actually validated with a real contractor commitment (Ben Barton, 2026-03-12). Changing it mid-Phase-1 violates Principle 2 (validate before building) because we'd be building around an unvalidated price. Flag any proposal to change pricing.

**How to apply:** Measure every dollar of ad spend and every hour of James's time against the implied per-accepted-bid return. Pushback triggers:
- CPL exceeds $60 for a sustained period → pause/reallocate
- Cost per accepted bid exceeds $400 → unit economics break, rethink targeting
- Contractor close rate drops below 15% → we either have bad leads or a weak contractor, diagnose
- Any proposal to charge contractors a flat fee instead of per-bid → requires contractor re-negotiation, flag

## Target Metrics

| Metric | Target | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Cost per lead (CPL) | <$60 | Industry benchmark for home services |
| Revenue per accepted bid | $1,000 | Contracted rate with Ben Barton, John Garcia |
| Contractor close rate | ~20% | Industry assumption; validate with real data |
| Cost per accepted bid | <$400 | ~5 leads × $60 + slippage buffer |
| Margin per accepted bid | ~$700 | $1,000 − $300 ad cost (at 20% close) |
| Advertising ROI | 2x+ | Revenue ÷ ad spend |

## Math Sanity Checks (use these to challenge proposals)
- 20 leads/mo × 20% close × $1,000 = $4,000 revenue
- At $60 CPL: 20 × $60 = $1,200 ad spend → $2,800 gross margin
- Phase 2 target: 20–50 leads/mo, $3K–$10K revenue
- Phase 1 break-even: 1 accepted bid covers the entire month of ad spend at target CPL

## What breaks the model
- CPL climbing to $100+ without a close-rate bump → margin collapses
- Contractor churn after 1–2 bids → acquisition cost per contractor becomes too high
- Leads with junk intent (tire-kickers) → close rate drops, everything downstream breaks
- Competitors bidding up keywords in our territory → Google CPL rises faster than Nextdoor/Facebook

## Decision Framework
If a proposal improves margin per accepted bid → green light.
If it widens top-of-funnel without affecting margin → neutral, evaluate on cost.
If it increases James's hours per week without proportional revenue → red flag by default.
