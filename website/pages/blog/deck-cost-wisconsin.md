## Article Content

If you are planning a deck project in Wisconsin, the first question on your mind is probably: how much is this going to cost?

The honest answer is that it depends. Material choice, deck size, site conditions, and where you live in the state all play a role. A basic pressure-treated wood deck and a premium composite deck with built-in lighting are two very different price tags.

This guide breaks down real 2026 deck costs for Wisconsin homeowners — with specific notes for those of us building in the [[Wausau]], [[Weston]], [[Schofield]], [[Mosinee]], and [[Merrill]] area.

---

### Average Deck Cost by Material

Material is the single biggest factor in your total cost. Here is what Wisconsin homeowners can expect to pay per square foot, including labor and materials:

| Material | Cost per Sq Ft (Installed) | Lifespan | Maintenance Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pressure-Treated Wood | $15 – $25 | 10 – 15 years | High (annual stain/seal) |
| Cedar | $25 – $40 | 15 – 20 years | Moderate (stain every 2-3 years) |
| Composite (Trex, TimberTech) | $30 – $60 | 25 – 50 years | Low (occasional wash) |
| PVC / Vinyl | $40 – $70 | 25 – 50 years | Very low |

These ranges include standard installation. Complex designs with multiple levels, curves, or built-in features will push toward the higher end.

### Cost by Project Type

Not every deck project starts from scratch. The type of work you need affects the price significantly.

**New deck build:** This is the most common project we see. You are building a deck where none exists. Expect the full per-square-foot costs listed above, plus footings, ledger board attachment, and potential grading work. A typical new 300-square-foot pressure-treated deck in Central Wisconsin runs $5,000 to $8,000 installed. The same size in composite is $10,000 to $18,000.

**Deck replacement:** If your existing deck is past its prime, full replacement involves demolition and disposal of the old structure before building new. Add $1,000 to $3,000 for demo depending on deck size and accessibility. The upside: your footings may still be good, which saves money.

**Deck repair and resurfacing:** If the frame is solid but the boards are worn, resurfacing is a cost-effective option. Replacing just the decking and railings typically costs 40 to 60 percent of a full build. Structural repairs to joists, posts, or footings are priced individually.

### Central Wisconsin-Specific Cost Factors

Building a deck in the [[Wausau]] area is not the same as building one in Milwaukee or Madison. Several regional factors affect your project cost.

**Frost footings:** In Central Wisconsin, deck footings must extend below the frost line — typically 48 inches deep in Marathon County. This is deeper than southern Wisconsin's 42-inch requirement. Deeper footings mean more concrete and labor, adding $200 to $600 to most projects.

**Seasonal labor demand:** Central Wisconsin has a compressed building season. Most deck projects happen between May and October. Contractors are busiest June through August, which can affect scheduling and pricing. Booking in early spring or fall often means better availability and sometimes lower labor rates.

**Material availability:** Pressure-treated lumber and cedar are readily available from local suppliers in the Wausau area. Composite and PVC materials sometimes have longer lead times, especially for specific colors or premium product lines. Ordering materials early avoids delays.

**Site conditions:** Many properties in the [[Wausau]], [[Weston]], and [[Mosinee]] area have sloped yards, high water tables, or challenging soil conditions. If your site needs extra grading, retaining, or drainage work, expect that to add $500 to $2,000 to your project.

### Hidden Costs Homeowners Forget

Every experienced builder knows these line items exist, but they catch many homeowners off guard during the quoting process.

**Building permits:** Most deck projects in Central Wisconsin require a building permit. In the City of Wausau, permits typically cost $50 to $150 depending on project scope. Skipping the permit is not worth the risk — unpermitted work can cause problems when you sell your home.

**Railings and balusters:** Standard railings are usually included in quotes, but upgrading to cable rail, glass panels, or aluminum balusters adds $20 to $50 per linear foot. On a 300-square-foot deck, that upgrade can total $1,500 to $3,000.

**Stairs:** A basic set of deck stairs is often included. But if your deck is elevated or you want wide stairs, wraparound stairs, or landings, the cost increases quickly. Complex stair systems can add $1,000 to $4,000.

**Demolition and disposal:** If you are replacing an old deck, removal and hauling away the old materials is an additional cost. Most contractors charge $1,000 to $3,000 depending on size.

**Skirting and finishing:** The area under an elevated deck needs skirting or lattice to close it off. This is often quoted separately at $5 to $15 per linear foot.

**Lighting and electrical:** Built-in deck lighting, outlet installation, or fan wiring requires an electrician. Budget $500 to $2,000 for basic deck lighting.

Online cost calculators and guides like this one give you a solid starting range, but every deck project is different. The only way to get a number you can actually budget around is to have a local builder look at your specific property, measure the space, and understand your design goals.

2. A builder visits your property to assess site conditions.
3. You receive a detailed written estimate with material and labor broken out.
4. You compare quotes from multiple builders (we recommend at least two or three).
