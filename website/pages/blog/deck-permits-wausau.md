## Article Content

**Short answer: Yes.** Most deck projects in Wausau, Wisconsin require a building permit from the City of Wausau Building Inspection Department.

If you are planning to build a new deck, replace an existing deck, or make structural changes to your current deck, you almost certainly need a permit before work begins. This is not optional — it is required by the Wisconsin Uniform Dwelling Code, and the City of Wausau enforces it.

This guide covers when you need a permit, when you might not, how to apply, what inspections look like, and what you need to know about setbacks and property lines.

---

### When a Permit IS Required

You need a building permit in the City of Wausau for any of the following deck projects:

- **New deck construction** — any new deck attached to a house or freestanding
- **Full deck replacement** — tearing down an existing deck and building a new one
- **Structural modifications** — changing the size, height, or layout of an existing deck
- **Decks more than 30 inches above grade** — this is the key threshold; most backyard decks off a main floor exceed this
- **Decks attached to the house** — any deck that connects to the home's structure via a ledger board
- **Adding a roof or pergola** over an existing deck
- **Significant railing changes** — especially if the deck is elevated

The 30-inch rule is the most important one to understand. If the walking surface of your deck is more than 30 inches above the ground at any point, a permit is required regardless of the deck's size. In Central Wisconsin, where many homes have walkout basements or sloped yards, most decks easily exceed this threshold.

### When a Permit May NOT Be Required

There are a few situations where you may not need a permit, though you should always verify with the city before starting work:

- **Minor surface repairs** — replacing a few damaged deck boards without changing the structure
- **Resurfacing only** — putting new decking over an existing frame that is structurally sound (no changes to footings, joists, or ledger)
- **Ground-level platforms** — freestanding platforms that sit directly on the ground and are less than 30 inches above grade at all points, with no attachment to the house
- **Cosmetic work** — staining, painting, or power washing your existing deck

Even in these cases, calling the City of Wausau Building Inspection Department to confirm is a smart move. A five-minute phone call can save you from a much bigger headache later.

---

### How to Apply for a Deck Permit in Wausau

The permit process in Wausau is straightforward. Here is what to expect:

**Step 1: Gather your documentation.** You will need a site plan showing where the deck will be on your property (with dimensions and distances from property lines), a deck design or construction plan showing the structure, and details on materials and footings.

**Step 2: Submit your application.** Applications go to the City of Wausau Building Inspection Department. You can typically submit in person at City Hall (407 Grant Street) or check if online submission is available. Include your site plan, deck plans, and the application form.

**Step 3: Pay the permit fee.** Deck permit fees in Wausau typically range from **$50 to $150** depending on the scope of the project. Fees are based on the estimated construction cost. This is a small price for legal compliance and peace of mind.

**Step 4: Wait for approval.** Review times vary, but most residential deck permits in Wausau are reviewed within 1 to 2 weeks. Simple projects can sometimes be approved faster.

**Step 5: Build according to your approved plans.** Once approved, you must build the deck as submitted. Any significant changes during construction may require a plan revision.

---

### What the Inspection Process Looks Like

After your permit is approved and construction begins, the city will require inspections at key stages. For a typical deck project in Wausau, expect these inspection points:

**Footing inspection:** Before pouring concrete, the inspector verifies that footing holes are dug to the proper depth (48 inches in Marathon County to meet frost line requirements) and are sized correctly for the load.

**Framing inspection:** After the structural frame is up — posts, beams, joists, and ledger board — the inspector checks that everything meets code for spacing, fasteners, connections, and structural integrity.

**Final inspection:** Once the deck is complete including decking, railings, and stairs, a final inspection confirms everything is built to code and matches the approved plans.

Inspections are scheduled through the Building Inspection Department, usually with 24 to 48 hours notice. A good contractor will coordinate all inspections as part of the project — you should not have to manage this yourself.

### Setback Requirements and Property Lines

One of the most common surprises homeowners encounter is setback requirements. Your deck cannot be built right up to your property line — the city requires minimum distances between structures and property boundaries.

In the City of Wausau, typical residential setback requirements include:

- **Rear yard setback:** Usually 25 to 30 feet from the rear property line, though decks may have reduced setback allowances compared to enclosed structures
- **Side yard setback:** Typically 5 to 10 feet from side property lines
- **Front yard setback:** Decks are rarely built in front yards, but if they are, front setbacks apply

These numbers vary based on your specific zoning district. Corner lots, lots on waterways, and properties in specific zoning overlays may have different requirements.

**Before you design your deck**, confirm your property lines and check your setback requirements with the city. A survey may be needed if property lines are unclear. Discovering a setback conflict after construction starts is expensive and stressful.

### What Happens If You Build Without a Permit?

Some homeowners consider skipping the permit to save time and money. This is a bad idea for several reasons:

- **Stop work orders:** If the city discovers unpermitted construction, they can issue a stop work order and require you to apply retroactively — often with penalty fees.
- **Fines:** Unpermitted work can result in fines from the city.
- **Insurance issues:** If someone is injured on an unpermitted deck, your homeowner's insurance may not cover the claim.
- **Problems when selling:** When you sell your home, the buyer's inspector or lender may flag unpermitted structures. This can delay or kill a sale, or force you to remove or re-permit the deck.
- **Safety:** The permit and inspection process exists to ensure your deck is structurally safe. A deck that collapses can cause serious injury.

The permit fee is a small fraction of your total project cost. It is always worth doing it right.

### What About Other Central Wisconsin Cities?

If you live outside the City of Wausau, the permit requirements are similar but not identical. Here is a quick overview:

**Village of [[Weston]]:** Requires building permits for decks. Contact the Weston Building Inspection Department. Requirements closely mirror Wausau's since both follow the Wisconsin Uniform Dwelling Code.

**City of [[Schofield]]:** Requires permits for deck construction. Check with Schofield City Hall for specific application procedures.

**City of [[Mosinee]]:** Building permits required for new decks and structural changes. Contact Mosinee Building Inspection.

**City of [[Merrill]]:** Permits required. Contact the City of Merrill Building Inspector.

Regardless of which Central Wisconsin city you are in, the Wisconsin Uniform Dwelling Code applies statewide. The 48-inch frost footing depth, structural requirements, and railing height standards are consistent across Marathon and Lincoln counties.
