## Article Content

Choosing the right deck material is one of the most important decisions you will make for your project. In Wisconsin, it matters even more than most places.

Our climate is uniquely hard on outdoor structures. Central Wisconsin sees over 100 freeze-thaw cycles per year, months of heavy snow cover, intense summer UV exposure, and dramatic temperature swings from -20 degrees in January to 90 degrees in July. A material that works fine in North Carolina will not necessarily hold up in [[Wausau]].

This guide compares the four most common deck materials — pressure-treated wood, cedar, composite, and PVC — specifically through the lens of what performs best in Wisconsin weather.

---

### Pressure-Treated Wood

Pressure-treated lumber is the most common deck material in Wisconsin, and for good reason: it is affordable, widely available, and strong. The wood is chemically treated to resist rot, insects, and fungal decay.

**Pros:**
- Lowest upfront cost ($15 to $25 per square foot installed)
- Readily available at every lumber yard in Central Wisconsin
- Easy to cut, shape, and work with for custom designs
- Accepts stain and paint well
- Strong structural performance

**Cons:**
- Requires annual maintenance — staining or sealing every 1 to 2 years
- Prone to warping, cracking, and splintering as it ages
- Freeze-thaw cycles accelerate splitting, especially if water penetrates unsealed end grain
- Shorter lifespan of 10 to 15 years before boards need replacing
- Can look worn and gray within 2 to 3 years without consistent maintenance

**Best for:** Budget-conscious builds where you are willing to invest time in annual upkeep. Also a smart choice if you plan to sell your home within a few years and want the lowest initial cost.

**Wisconsin reality check:** Pressure-treated wood takes a beating in our climate. Homeowners who keep up with staining get solid performance. Those who skip a year or two see rapid deterioration — cracking, splintering, and gray discoloration that is hard to reverse.

---

### Cedar

Cedar is a step up from pressure-treated wood in both appearance and natural durability. It contains natural oils that resist rot and insects without chemical treatment, and its warm reddish-brown color is hard to beat.

**Pros:**
- Beautiful natural appearance with rich grain patterns
- Naturally resistant to rot, decay, and insect damage
- Lighter weight than pressure-treated wood, easier to handle
- Does not splinter as aggressively as pressure-treated wood
- No chemical treatment required

**Cons:**
- Higher cost than pressure-treated ($25 to $40 per square foot installed)
- Still requires staining or sealing every 2 to 3 years to maintain color
- Will weather to a silver-gray if left untreated (some homeowners prefer this)
- Softer wood — more susceptible to dents and surface damage from furniture
- Can be harder to source in specific grades in Central Wisconsin

**Best for:** Homeowners who want a natural wood look and are willing to do moderate maintenance. Cedar decks have a warm, premium appearance that composite cannot fully replicate.

**Wisconsin reality check:** Cedar handles our moisture well thanks to its natural oils, but it is not maintenance-free. The freeze-thaw cycle still demands regular sealing to prevent water from getting into the grain. Expect a 15 to 20-year lifespan with proper care.

### Composite Decking (Trex, TimberTech)

Composite decking is made from a blend of wood fibers and plastic polymers, creating a material that looks like wood but requires almost no maintenance. Leading brands include Trex, TimberTech, and Fiberon. This category has improved dramatically over the past decade.

**Pros:**
- Very low maintenance — no staining, sealing, or painting required
- Will not splinter, rot, or warp
- 25 to 50-year manufacturer warranties
- Resists moisture penetration — critical for freeze-thaw performance
- Wide range of colors and textures that mimic natural wood
- Consistent appearance with no knots or grain variation
- Resists fading with capped polymer technology

**Cons:**
- Higher upfront cost ($30 to $60 per square foot installed)
- Can get hot underfoot in direct summer sun (darker colors especially)
- Heavier than wood — requires proper structural support
- Cannot be refinished or sanded if deeply scratched
- Some lower-end composites can still stain from mold or tannins

**Best for:** Homeowners who want a long-lasting, low-maintenance deck and are willing to pay more upfront. Composite is the best all-around performer for Wisconsin's climate. The lack of water absorption means freeze-thaw cycles have minimal impact on the material.

**Wisconsin reality check:** This is where composite truly shines. The number one enemy of deck materials in Wisconsin is water getting inside and then expanding when it freezes. Composite resists moisture penetration at the surface level, which dramatically reduces freeze-thaw damage. Most of the premium composite decks we see in Central Wisconsin still look great after 15 to 20 years with nothing more than an occasional wash.

---

### PVC / Vinyl Decking

PVC decking is 100 percent synthetic — no wood fibers at all. It is the most weather-resistant option available and requires virtually zero maintenance.

**Pros:**
- Completely waterproof — zero moisture absorption
- Will not rot, warp, crack, splinter, or support mold growth
- Extremely low maintenance — just wash occasionally
- Lightweight compared to composite
- Excellent resistance to staining
- Best freeze-thaw performance of any deck material

**Cons:**
- Highest cost ($40 to $70 per square foot installed)
- Can feel less natural underfoot — some homeowners find it too "plastic"
- Limited color options compared to composite
- Can expand and contract noticeably with temperature changes
- Fewer brand options than composite

**Best for:** Homeowners with the budget for premium materials who want absolute maximum lifespan and minimum maintenance. Also excellent for lakefront or waterfront properties where moisture exposure is extreme.

**Wisconsin reality check:** PVC is bulletproof against Wisconsin weather. If budget is not a constraint, it is objectively the most durable choice. However, most Central Wisconsin homeowners find that high-end composite delivers 90 percent of the performance at a meaningfully lower price.

### Wisconsin-Specific Factors to Consider

**Freeze-thaw cycles:** Central Wisconsin experiences over 100 freeze-thaw cycles annually. Every time water penetrates a material, freezes, expands, and thaws, it causes micro-damage. Over years, this is what causes wood to crack, splinter, and deteriorate. Materials that resist water penetration — composite and PVC — hold up dramatically better.

**Snow load:** Wisconsin decks carry heavy snow for months. All four materials handle weight fine structurally (that is a framing issue, not a decking issue), but trapped moisture under snow accelerates damage to wood surfaces. Composite and PVC do not absorb that moisture.

**UV exposure:** Wisconsin summers are sunnier than people expect. UV breaks down wood fibers and causes graying. Premium composite with polymer capping resists UV fading far better than any natural wood.

**Temperature swings:** From subzero winter nights to 90-degree summer afternoons, materials expand and contract. PVC moves the most, which is why proper gapping during installation is critical. Composite and wood handle temperature changes well when installed correctly.

### Our Recommendation for Central Wisconsin

For most homeowners in the [[Wausau]] area, **composite decking is the best overall choice**. The higher upfront cost pays for itself within 5 to 8 years when you factor in the staining, sealing, and board replacement that wood decks require.

If budget is the primary constraint, pressure-treated wood still builds a perfectly good deck — just plan for annual maintenance and a shorter lifespan.

If you want the absolute premium experience and have the budget, PVC is the most durable option available.
