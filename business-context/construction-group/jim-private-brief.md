## 1. The headline verdict

**The SBG structure is better for you than the merger we drafted first, and I would do it.** You traded shared upside for protection and control, and for your position that is the right trade. You keep CWDB, you keep the lead engine, you keep your own job profit, and you do not dump your most valuable asset into a pot you only own a third of. The phased approach (co-op now, merge only if it works) is exactly how a cautious person should enter a partnership: prove it with low risk before pooling real money.

**The one thing that did not change: do not quit your $140,000 job cold in June on three months of reserve.** The structure is safer, but your personal cash timing is the same risk it was this morning.

## 2. Why this structure protects you

- **Your asset stays yours.** The engine and the CWDB brand feed only your jobs and are not licensed or contributed to SBG. If the partnership ever sours, you walk away with the whole machine intact. That is a far stronger position than contributing it into a shared OpCo.
- **Your profit stays yours in Phase A.** You keep CWDB's job profit. Whoever books more keeps more, and you control the engine that books the most.
- **The risk of combining is low.** Nobody pools profit yet. You are sharing crews and equipment, not your book. If it does not work, you unwind it and still have CWDB.

## 4. Your leverage and the things to protect

- **You already protected the engine.** Keeping it 100 percent CWDB's was the right call and it is your single biggest piece of leverage. Hold that line.
- **The leverage shifts to Phase B.** When you pool, your engine starts feeding everyone, and you equalize three businesses that earned differently in Phase A. That is where you can quietly give or get a lot of value. So the most important thing you can do tonight and in the term sheet is nail down the Phase B valuation and equalization method and the engine re-pricing NOW, in calm times. Do not move to Phase B on a handshake.
- **Watch the wage rate.** The SBG W-2 wage is your pay for labor. Make sure the loaded billing rate SBG charges the LLCs (around $100 per hour) and the wage you draw are set so SBG is sustainable and you are paid fairly. The old $80 per hour was break-even once loaded.

## 5. Two tax and money flags that are easy to miss

- **The S-corp election for SBG-Labor is not optional if you want real W-2 paychecks.** A partner cannot be a W-2 employee of a partnership they own. If SBG-Labor were a plain partnership, your wage would be a guaranteed payment taxed harder. Make sure the attorney and CPA set SBG-Labor up as an S-corp. This is the structural catch in the whole plan.
- **Equipment leases between your own companies carry Wisconsin sales tax.** Renting gear from SBG-Equipment to the LLCs is a taxable rental, unlike construction labor. This is a genuinely separate transaction from your standing "no sales tax on CWDB revenue" decision, so it does not contradict that call, but SBG-Equipment must register and remit. Confirm with the DOR before the first lease invoice or it becomes a back-tax problem later.
