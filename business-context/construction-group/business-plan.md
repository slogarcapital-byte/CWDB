# SBG Construction Group

## Partnership Business Plan and Proposal

**Entity group:** SBG Construction Group (Slogar, Barton, Garcia)
**Partners:** Jim Slogar (Central Wisconsin Deck Builders, LLC) · Ben Barton (Barton Builders LLC) · John Garcia (John Garcia Construction LLC)
**Prepared:** 2026-06-17
**Status:** Proposal for partner alignment. Companion documents: `financial-model.md`, `legal-term-sheet.md`, `inter-company-agreements.md`, `due-diligence-checklist.md`.

---

> This is a plan to align three trade businesses on one shared structure. Every number here is a labeled, illustrative starting point for discussion, not a final term. Nothing binds anyone until a Wisconsin attorney drafts the agreements and all three of us sign.

---

## 1. Executive summary

Three trade businesses in Central Wisconsin share the expensive, hard parts of running a construction company while each of us keeps our own business. We do NOT merge our companies on day one. Instead we form SBG: a shared group that owns the crews, the equipment, and (later) the real estate, and rents them to each of our three LLCs as jobs come in. Each of us keeps signing our own jobs, keeping our own customers, and keeping our own profit. SBG is how we get the buying power, the shared crews, and the systems of a big company without giving up our own books on day one.

We get there in two stages:

1. **Phase A (now, the next 1 to 2 years):** a shared-services co-op. Each LLC runs its own jobs and keeps its own profit. SBG owns the crews and equipment and bills each LLC at fair market rates to use them. We prove we work well together with real money on the line, but with low risk, because nobody's profit is pooled yet.
2. **Phase B (1 to 2 years out):** if Phase A works, we transition to a true merger where job profit pools and splits one third. We decide the rules for that transition now, in calm times, not in the middle of it.

The rest of this plan covers what each of us keeps, what SBG holds, how the money and pay work, how we govern it, the legal and tax setup, the transition to Phase B, and the next steps.

## 2. The opportunity

- **The demand is already here.** Ben and John are booked into 2027, and steady homeowner leads and live quotes are already coming in. The constraint on all of us is capacity, crews, equipment, and systems, not finding work.
- **The expensive parts are what hold a trade back.** Crews, trucks, lifts, and a real shop are exactly what a single trade business struggles to afford and keep busy. Shared across three books of work, they stay busy and pay for themselves.
- **We stay our own bosses while we test it.** This structure lets us combine the costly infrastructure first and only merge the businesses later, once we have proven the relationship and the numbers.

## 3. The vision: keep your own business, share the expensive parts

Each of us keeps our own LLC, our own customers, our own brand, and our own job profit. SBG owns and runs the shared muscle:

| Stays with each partner (the three LLCs) | Shared in SBG |
|---|---|
| Your customers and your brand | The W-2 field crews |
| Your signed jobs and your job profit | The equipment (trucks, tools, lifts) |
| Your own contractor license and insurance | The real estate (a shop or yard, later) |
| Your own lead sources | The systems and buying power |

SBG rents its crews and equipment to whichever LLC has the work. You pay SBG for what you use, and you keep what is left.

## 4. The two stages

### Phase A: the shared-services co-op (now)

- Each LLC signs its own homeowner contracts, pulls its own permits, carries its own insurance, and KEEPS ITS OWN JOB PROFIT.
- SBG owns the crews and equipment and bills each LLC at fair market rates when it uses them.
- Whoever books more work keeps more profit. There is no pooling yet, so the risk of combining is low.
- We run real jobs through this structure and learn whether we work well together with money on the line.

### Phase B: the merger (1 to 2 years out, only if Phase A works)

- Job profit pools and splits one third. This is the true merger.
- We agree NOW, in writing, on the rules: what triggers the move, how we value and equalize three businesses that earned differently in Phase A, and whether the three LLCs collapse into one or stay as brands.
- Deciding the mechanism in calm times is the whole point. We do not want to negotiate the hardest questions in the middle of the transition.

## 5. The SBG entity structure

SBG is not one company. It is three separate companies, each owned one third by the three of us as individuals, kept separate so a problem in one does not sink the others.

- **SBG-Labor:** employs the W-2 crews and all three of us, and leases that labor to the LLCs. (For tax reasons it is set up as an S-corp so each of us can draw a real W-2 paycheck for the hours we work. See Section 9.)
- **SBG-Equipment:** owns the equipment and leases it to the LLCs.
- **SBG-RealEstate:** formed now but kept dormant until we are ready to buy a shop or yard. For now we use our own space.

Real estate sits in its own company on purpose, so that a jobsite injury claim against the crews can never reach the building we own. Equipment is separated for the same reason. This is standard practice in the trades and it protects all three of us.

## 6. How the money and pay work in Phase A

> The principle: each of us earns from our own work and our own jobs. SBG is a shared cost-and-asset center, not a pot we split, until Phase B.

Each partner's income in Phase A has two parts:

1. **Your own LLC's job profit.** You sign the job, you keep the profit, the same as today. Whoever books and runs more work earns more.
2. **An hourly W-2 wage from SBG** for the hours you personally work, whether on your own job or helping on another partner's job. SBG pays you like an employee for your labor.

SBG bills each LLC at fair market rates for the crews and equipment it uses (a loaded labor rate plus an equipment lease rate). SBG's own profit is REINVESTED in the first 1 to 2 years, into more equipment and a cash reserve, rather than paid out. This is how the shared muscle grows. SBG does still send each of us enough cash to cover the tax on our share (a tax distribution), because SBG's profit passes through to us on paper.

## 7. Each partner keeps their own customers and lead sources

Each of us keeps our own customers, our own brand, and our own way of finding work. SBG does not market, generate leads, or own any customer relationship: it only supplies the crews and equipment. Whatever each LLC brings in, that LLC keeps in Phase A. Any prior lead-sharing arrangement among us is wound down, so each of us runs our own customer acquisition.

## 8. Governance and protection

Three equal owners with no tiebreaker is how partnerships freeze, so we solve it deliberately for the SBG companies. Full mechanics are in `legal-term-sheet.md`.

- **Lanes:** each of us runs one lane (Jim sales, marketing, and finance; John customer service, estimating, and scheduling; Ben field operations and the crews). The lane owner decides inside their lane day to day.
- **Major decisions** need at least two of three; the biggest decisions (selling, dissolving, admitting an owner, the Phase B merger itself) need all three.
- **Deadlock:** written positions, a meeting, then mediation, then a pre-agreed neutral advisor for a true operating tie. A buy-sell trigger guarantees the company never stays frozen.
- **Nobody gets trapped:** a buy-sell with right of first refusal, defined buyouts for exit, death, disability, and removal for cause, and a closed back door so no one can sell their stake to a stranger.
- **Transparency:** because finance sits with Jim, all three get monthly statements, open books, and bank visibility.

## 9. The legal and tax setup

The detail is in `financial-model.md` and `legal-term-sheet.md`. The headlines:

- **SBG-Labor is taxed as an S-corp.** This is what lets each of us draw a real W-2 wage from SBG for our hours. (In a plain partnership, an owner cannot be a true W-2 employee, and the pay would be taxed harder.) SBG-Equipment and SBG-RealEstate are simpler partnership-taxed LLCs.
- **Each LLC keeps its own license and insurance.** Each of us is the prime contractor on our own jobs, so each LLC keeps its own Dwelling Contractor Certification, its own Qualifier, and its own general liability. Jim still needs to finish his license to sign builds. SBG itself does not contract with homeowners, so it likely needs no contractor certification, which we confirm with the state.
- **Workers' comp lives in SBG-Labor** and covers all the crews and all three of us, coordinated so an injury on any LLC's job is covered.
- **Equipment leases carry Wisconsin sales tax.** Renting equipment between SBG-Equipment and the LLCs is a taxable rental in Wisconsin, unlike construction labor billed to a homeowner. SBG-Equipment registers and remits that tax. We confirm the treatment with the Wisconsin Department of Revenue before the first lease invoice.
- **The inter-company agreements are written and market-priced.** The labor lease, the equipment lease, and the workers' comp coordination are real contracts at fair market rates, both for tax and for legal protection. See `inter-company-agreements.md`.

## 10. The financial shape

The detail and the assumptions are in `financial-model.md`. The shape:

- **Startup capital is equal cash.** Each partner puts in roughly the same cash (an illustrative $30,000 to $45,000 each, to be sized against the actual equipment list and payroll float). SBG uses it to buy its own equipment and to float payroll, since crews are paid weekly while the LLCs are billed and collect later. If a partner wants to sell existing gear into SBG, that is a separate fair-market sale, not a contribution, which keeps ownership cleanly equal. 
- **No real estate capital yet.** The real-estate company is dormant, so there is no facility purchase or mortgage in Phase A.
- **SBG runs lean and reinvests.** Its margin builds the equipment fleet and the reserve rather than paying out, which is how the shared infrastructure scales.

## 11. The Phase B transition (decide it now)

The move from co-op to merger is the most valuable thing to settle while everyone is calm and aligned. The term sheet builds in:

- **A trigger** for when we move (recommend a unanimous decision).
- **A valuation and equalization method** for three businesses that earned differently in Phase A, so pooling is fair.
- **The end state of the three LLCs:** collapse into one operating company, or keep them as brands under the merged company.

## 12. Phased growth

- **Phase 0, now:** form the three SBG entities, sign the inter-company agreements, put in the equal cash, and run live jobs through the co-op.
- **Phase 1:** prove the relationship and the numbers in Phase A. Build the crews, the equipment, and the reserve.
- **Phase 2:** if it works, execute the Phase B merger on the pre-agreed rules.
- **Phase 3:** scale the merged company with more W-2 crews and, when ready, buy the shop or yard through the real-estate company.
- **Phase 4:** expand the proven model into adjacent Central Wisconsin markets.

## 13. Next steps and the ask

1. Agree in principle on the structure: three SBG entities owned one third each, the three LLCs stay independent in Phase A, equal cash in, SBG bills at market and reinvests, the Phase B rules decided now.
2. Each of us completes the due-diligence disclosure (companion checklist) so we go in with eyes open.
3. Confirm the open decisions: the equal cash amount, the loaded labor and equipment rates, and the Phase B transition rules.
4. Engage a licensed Wisconsin attorney to draft the operating agreements, the inter-company agreements, and the buy-sell, and confirm the tax setup (including the SBG-Labor S-corp election) with the CPA.
5. Confirm the sales-tax treatment on equipment leases with the Wisconsin Department of Revenue, and the licensing split with the state, before the first job runs through the structure.
