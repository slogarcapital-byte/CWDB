Why now

The work is here. The expensive parts hold us back.

  • The demand is booked. Ben and John are scheduled into 2027, and homeowner leads and live quotes are already coming in. The constraint is crews, equipment, and systems, not finding work.
  • Crews and equipment are what a single trade can't keep busy. Shared across three books of work, they stay busy and pay for themselves.
  • We stay our own bosses while we test it. Combine the costly infrastructure first. Merge the businesses only after we prove the relationship and the numbers.
The vision

Keep your own business. Share the expensive parts.

Stays with each of us

Your customers and brand. Your signed jobs and your job profit. Your own license and insurance. Your own lead sources.

Shared in SBG

The W-2 field crews. The equipment (trucks, tools, lifts). The real estate (a shop or yard, later). The systems and buying power.

SBG rents its crews and equipment to whichever of us has the work. You pay SBG for what you use, and you keep what is left.

The model

Two stages: prove it, then merge.

Phase A · now · 1 to 2 years

Shared-services co-op

Each LLC signs its own jobs and keeps its own profit. SBG owns the crews and equipment and bills each LLC at market to use them. Whoever books more keeps more. Low risk, because nobody's profit is pooled yet.

Phase B · later · only if it works

The merger

Job profit pools and splits one third. We agree the rules now, in calm times: what triggers it, and how we value and equalize what each of the three businesses brings.

Three separate SBG companies, owned one third each by the three of us. Kept separate on purpose: a jobsite claim against the crews can never reach the building or the equipment. Standard trades practice.

How the money works (Phase A)

You earn from your own work and your own jobs.

Your two paychecks

1. Your LLC's job profit. You sign it, you keep it, same as today. Whoever books more earns more.

2. A W-2 wage from SBG for the hours you personally work, on any job, yours or a partner's.

SBG itself

Bills each LLC at fair market rates for crews and equipment. Reinvests its profit for the first 1 to 2 years into more equipment and a reserve, instead of paying out. That is how the shared muscle grows.

SBG is a shared cost-and-asset center in Phase A, not a pot we split. Pooling comes in Phase B.

Governance

How three equal owners actually decide.

  • Lanes: Jim runs sales, marketing, finance. John runs customer service, estimating, scheduling. Ben runs field operations and the crews. The lane owner decides day to day.
  • Major decisions: at least two of three agree. The biggest (selling, dissolving, the Phase B merger) need all three.
  • Deadlock: written positions, a meeting, mediation, then a pre-agreed neutral advisor breaks a true tie. A buy-sell trigger means the company never stays frozen.
Protection

Nobody gets trapped. Nobody gets robbed.

  • Buy-sell with right of first refusal: no one sells to a stranger without offering the other two first, at a pre-agreed value.
  • Covered events: exit, death, disability, removal for cause, each with a defined buyout.
  • The back door is closed: you cannot route around the rules by selling your shell company.
  • Transparency: monthly statements, open books, and bank visibility for all three, since finance sits with Jim.
The operational heart

Three written agreements make it work.

Labor lease

SBG-Labor to each LLC

Supplies crews at a market rate (wages + payroll tax + workers' comp + overhead + margin). The LLC's licensed Qualifier directs the work.

Equipment lease

SBG-Equipment to each LLC

Leases the gear at a market rate. Note: Wisconsin sales tax applies to equipment rentals, and SBG-Equipment remits it.

Workers' comp coordination

protects all three LLCs

SBG-Labor's coverage is coordinated so a leased-worker injury on any LLC's job is covered and barred against that LLC.

These are real, market-priced contracts between our own companies, required for both tax and legal protection.

Illustrative, to be sized against real numbers

What it takes to stand up SBG.

  • Equal cash in: roughly $30,000 to $45,000 each (to be sized against the actual equipment list and payroll float).
  • It funds two things: SBG buys its own equipment, and it floats payroll (crews are paid weekly while the LLCs are billed and collect later).
  • No real estate cost yet: the real-estate company stays dormant. We use our own space for now.
  • SBG runs lean and reinvests, building the equipment fleet and the reserve rather than paying out.

Selling existing gear into SBG is a separate fair-market sale, not a contribution, which keeps ownership cleanly equal.

Legal and tax setup

Set up right from day one.

  • SBG-Labor is an S-corp, which is what lets each of us draw a real W-2 paycheck for our hours.
  • Each LLC keeps its own license and insurance: its own Dwelling Contractor Certification, Qualifier, and general liability. Jim still needs to finish his license to sign builds.
  • Workers' comp lives in SBG-Labor and covers every crew member and all three of us.
  • Equipment leases carry Wisconsin sales tax; SBG-Equipment registers and remits it. We confirm with the state before the first lease invoice.
The merger, decided now

Settle the hard questions while we are friends.

  • The trigger: what moves us from co-op to merger (recommend a unanimous decision).
  • The valuation: how we fairly value and equalize what each of the three businesses brings to the table at pooling.
  • The end state: whether the three LLCs collapse into one company or stay as brands.

The whole point of Phase A is to earn the trust to do Phase B. We write the rules for it now, not in the middle of it.

The path

Phased growth.

  • Now: form the three SBG entities, sign the inter-company agreements, put in the equal cash, run live jobs through the co-op.
  • Phase A: prove the relationship and the numbers. Build the crews, equipment, and reserve.
  • Phase B: if it works, execute the merger on the pre-agreed rules.
  • Then scale: more crews, and buy the shop or yard through the real-estate company when ready. Expand into adjacent markets.
