# CWDB Brand Voice & Positioning

**Status:** Approved — March 12, 2026
**Source:** Brand voice interview with founder
**Companion to:** `Central Wisconsin Deck Builders - Brand Style Guide.md`

This document covers *how we sound* and *why we sound that way*. The style guide covers how we look. Use both together.

---

## 1. Positioning Statement

[[Central Wisconsin Deck Builders LLC|Central Wisconsin Deck Builders]] is the fastest, most reliable way for Central Wisconsin homeowners to get connected with a vetted local deck contractor — without the research, guesswork, or waiting. We exist for the homeowner who doesn't want to spend three weeks chasing contractors. We do that work so they don't have to.

We are not a contractor. We are not a directory. We are the trusted middleman who makes the process simple and certain.

---

## 2. The Core Customer: The Stressed Delegator

**Who they are:**
A homeowner in the [[Wausau]] area, 35–60, who wants a deck built or replaced but dreads the contractor search. They've probably heard horror stories — contractors who ghost you, bids that never arrive, no-shows. They have a budget. They have a timeline. They just want someone reliable to show up.

**What they fear:**
- Getting ripped off
- Wasting time on contractors who never call back
- Making the wrong choice and regretting it

**What they need to hear:**
- "This is easy."
- "You won't get burned."
- "Here's exactly what happens next."

**What they do NOT need:**
- Design inspiration (they're not here to dream, they're here to act)
- Pressure tactics (they're already anxious — urgency backfires on the landing page)
- Jargon or corporate language (they want to feel like a neighbor helped them)

**Implication for every piece of content:** Lead with relief, not excitement. The message is "we've got you," not "look at this amazing deck you could have."

---

## 3. Brand Tone: The 4–5 Zone

On a scale of 1 (bare-knuckle trade) to 10 (premium luxury craftsmanship), CWDB is a **4–5**.

**What 4–5 means in practice:**
- Trade-credible. We speak like someone who knows decks, not like a marketing department.
- Approachable. A homeowner writing a $15K check should feel like they're dealing with a professional, not a roughneck.
- Clean and confident. No frills, but not rough either. Milwaukee Tool is the visual reference — strong, modern, no nonsense.

**What 4–5 rules out:**

| Too Low (1–3) | Too High (7–10) |
|---|---|
| Raw, intimidating job-site language | Aspirational lifestyle copy ("your outdoor oasis awaits") |
| Talking only to tradespeople | Premium pricing language ("investment," "bespoke") |
| Ignoring aesthetics entirely | Delicate or thin typography, soft color palette |
| Macho posturing | Over-designed, high-end brochure feel |

## 4. The Hammer vs. Handshake Rule

The burnt orange signals urgency and action — but CWDB doesn't always move at the same speed. The right energy depends on where the customer is in the funnel.

### Ads — The Hammer
Cold traffic needs a push. Someone scrolling Facebook or Google isn't thinking about their deck right now. The ad's only job is to stop the scroll and drive the click.

- Lead with the problem or the urgency
- High contrast, punchy copy, direct CTA
- Orange energy is full throttle here
- Example: *"Wisconsin summer books up fast. Don't wait."* / *"Tired of contractors who never call back?"*

### Landing Page — The Handshake
Warm traffic is already interested. They clicked. Now they're evaluating whether to trust us. Pressure here backfires — it reads as salesy. What earns the form submit is calm certainty.

- Lead with relief and clarity
- Explain exactly what happens after they submit
- Reduce friction at every sentence
- Orange is reserved for the CTA button only — not the tone
- Example: *"Fill out the form below and a local contractor will reach out within 24 hours."*

### Contractor Outreach — Peer Respect
Contractors are not homeowners. They evaluate us on ROI, not reassurance. Speak like a business peer who respects their time.

- Lead with the value proposition, immediately
- Use numbers where possible (lead volume, project size, close rate math)
- No hype, no fluff — they'll see through it
- Example: *"Average deck job is $10K–$30K. You pay us 3–10% only when you get paid."*

## 5. The Three Anti-Words

CWDB is deliberately **not** these things. When reviewing any piece of copy, run this check:

### Generic
Generic looks like: national chain language, cookie-cutter headlines, nothing that couldn't apply to any contractor in any state.

**Don't:** "Get your free estimate today from our team of experienced professionals."
**Do:** "Wausau area contractors, ready to quote your project."

The fix: Specificity. City names, regional references, honest language. Generic is often caused by removing all the details to sound "professional."

### Salesy
Salesy looks like: pressure, hype, claims that are too big, excitement that feels manufactured.

**Don't:** "HURRY — limited spots available! Don't miss out on this incredible opportunity!"
**Do:** "Summer fills up fast. Getting your quote in early means more options."

The fix: Translate urgency into a real reason. Replace manufactured scarcity with honest timing.

### Cheap
Cheap looks like: emphasizing low price, discount framing, anything that signals corner-cutting.

**Don't:** "Compare quotes and save big on your deck!"
**Do:** "Get matched with a trusted local contractor. Free, no obligation."

The fix: Lead with trust and speed, not price. The Stressed Delegator is not primarily shopping for the lowest bid — they're shopping for certainty. Price-forward messaging attracts the wrong customer and undermines contractor relationships.

## 6. Messaging Hierarchy

When building any page, ad, or outreach piece, lead in this order:

1. **Speed** — You'll hear back fast. This is the core differentiator. Lead with it.
2. **Trust** — These are vetted, local contractors. Not strangers from the internet.
3. **Local** — Central Wisconsin. Your city. Your neighborhood. Not a national platform.

Speed unlocks the click. Trust earns the form submit. Local closes the deal.

**Why this order matters:** Homeowners don't care about "local" if they don't believe it'll be fast. And they don't care about speed if they don't trust the people on the other end. The sequence builds the argument.

## 7. Voice in Practice

Short rewrites showing the principles applied.

**Before (generic, corporate):**
> "Our team of experienced deck building professionals is ready to help you create the outdoor living space of your dreams. Contact us today for a free consultation."

**After (CWDB voice):**
> "Local deck builders in Wausau, ready to quote your project. Fill out one short form — hear back within 24 hours."

---

**Before (too high — luxury/aspirational):**
> "Transform your backyard into a stunning outdoor oasis. Discover the craftsmanship of Central Wisconsin's premier deck building partners."

**After (CWDB voice):**
> "Get a free deck quote from a vetted local contractor. No guesswork. No waiting weeks to hear back."

---

**Before (too salesy — landing page):**
> "Spots are filling up FAST! Don't miss your chance to get a quote this season. Request yours NOW before it's too late!"

**After (CWDB voice):**
> "Most contractors in Central Wisconsin are booked 4–8 weeks out by mid-spring. Getting your quote in now means more options and a better build date."

---

**Before (cheap-framing):**
> "Compare multiple deck quotes and save thousands. Get the best price on your new deck!"

**After (CWDB voice):**
> "We connect you with licensed, local deck builders. One form. Fast response. No pressure."

## 8. Priority Application (Phase 1)

Not all touchpoints are equal right now. Design energy should go here first:

1. **Contractor outreach materials** (`/sales/outreach/`) — Phase 1 validation requires a paying contractor before ad spend begins. These materials must work.
2. **The [[Webflow]] landing page** (`/website/`) — Once a contractor is signed, this is where homeowner trust gets built and leads get captured.

Ads are third priority. Don't spend on traffic until the landing page converts.
