## PLAIN-ENGLISH EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

You are now both a **lead seller** (you generate homeowner deck leads and sell them to contractors at $1,000 per accepted bid) and a **direct builder** (you bid and build deck jobs yourself). The same two contractors, Ben Barton and John Garcia, will play **two roles**: they keep **buying leads** from you, and as your volume grows they will also do work **for you as subcontractors**.

The good news on the pure legal question: **nothing in your signed Contractor Lead Purchase Agreements stops CWDB from building its own jobs.** There is no non-compete and no exclusivity binding CWDB. Section 3 of the agreement only restricts how leads are shared; it does not promise the contractors that CWDB will refrain from building. As a pure legal-prohibition matter, this is **LOW** risk.

The thing to manage is **conduct**, not prohibition. Wisconsin reads an **implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing** into contracts, so even though you are allowed to build, you should not use the lead relationship in a way that is unfair or deceptive toward the contractors or toward homeowners. That is a **MEDIUM** risk you manage with discipline: keep the two relationships clearly separated, account honestly when you build a job from your own lead, and never mislead a homeowner about who is actually building their deck.

The rest of this memo gives you the rules of the road: which contract governs which interaction, how to handle lead economics when you build internally, how to disclose to homeowners, and how confidentiality cuts (mostly it protects you, but you must guard against subs misusing your data).

## 1. NO CONTRACTUAL OR STATUTORY BAR TO CWDB BUILDING ITS OWN JOBS (LOW)

The signed Contractor Lead Purchase Agreements contain **no non-compete** and **no exclusivity provision that restricts CWDB**. Specifically:

- **Section 3 (Lead Exclusivity)** governs only how **leads** are distributed. Section 3.1 makes leads **non-exclusive** by default, and Section 3.3 confirms the agreement does not restrict the **contractor** from generating its own leads. Nothing in Section 3, or anywhere else in the agreement, promises the contractor that **CWDB** will refrain from bidding or building.
- **Section 10 (Independent Contractor)** confirms the parties are independent businesses, not partners or joint venturers. There is no fiduciary duty between CWDB and the contractors that would constrain CWDB from competing.
- There is no Wisconsin statute that bars a lead-generation business from also performing the underlying trade. A licensed, insured deck builder may build decks.

As a pure **legal-prohibition** question, CWDB building its own jobs is **LOW** risk. The amendment (companion document) states this expressly so there is no ambiguity with Ben or John.

## 2. THE IMPLIED COVENANT OF GOOD FAITH AND FAIR DEALING (MEDIUM)

Wisconsin reads an **implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing** into every contract. The covenant does not let you rewrite the deal, and it does not create a non-compete that the parties never agreed to. But it does mean a party cannot exercise its contractual discretion in a way that **deprives the other party of the benefit of the bargain** through bad faith.

Why this matters here: the lead-purchase agreement gives CWDB broad discretion to **pause, limit, or redirect leads** (Sections 2.5 and 6.5). That discretion is yours to use for legitimate business reasons. The risk is using it in a way that looks like bad faith, for example, steering the **best** homeowner leads to your own in-house build crew while feeding the contractors only the weak leads, in a way that contradicts what the contractors were reasonably led to expect when they signed up to buy leads.

This is **MEDIUM** risk and it is managed by conduct, not by changing the contract:

- Have a **legitimate, documentable business reason** whenever you redirect leads or build in-house.
- Do not make verbal or marketing promises to contractors (about lead volume, quality, or "first dibs") that your in-house building then quietly undercuts. The agreement's integration clause (Section 16.3) helps, but good-faith conduct still matters.
- Keep the lead-buy relationship and the build relationship **visibly separate** so it is clear you are running two honest lines, not gaming one against the other.

> If you ever plan to systematically reserve premium leads for your own crew, document the policy and consider disclosing the general practice to your lead-buying contractors, so expectations are aligned. Confirm with a Wisconsin attorney before adopting such a policy.

## 3. ROLE OVERLAP: BEN AND JOHN AS BOTH LEAD-BUYERS AND SUBCONTRACTORS

Ben Barton (Barton Builders LLC) and John Garcia (John Garcia Construction, LLC) will occupy **two distinct relationships** with the LLC. Keep them clean:

### 3.1 Which contract governs which interaction

| Interaction | Governing contract | Money flow | Who is the customer |
|---|---|---|---|
| Contractor buys a homeowner lead from CWDB and wins the job | **Contractor Lead Purchase Agreement** (as amended) | Contractor pays CWDB $1,000 per accepted bid | The homeowner is the contractor's customer; CWDB is the lead seller |
| Contractor performs build/stain work for CWDB on a CWDB job | **Subcontractor Agreement** (separate, see docs/legal/templates/subcontractor-agreement-template.md) | CWDB pays the contractor for the subcontracted work | The homeowner is **CWDB's** customer; the contractor is CWDB's sub |

The two relationships are **independent**. A single homeowner project should fall into exactly one column, never both. If CWDB sells the lead and the contractor builds it, that is the lead-purchase column. If CWDB wins the homeowner and hires the contractor to help build, that is the subcontractor column.

### 3.2 Practical separation rules

- **Never blend the two in one document or invoice.** A lead-purchase invoice (contractor pays CWDB) and a subcontractor payment (CWDB pays contractor) must be separate transactions with separate paperwork.
- **Do not net the two against each other** without explicit written agreement and CPA/attorney review. Netting "you owe me a $1,000 lead fee, I owe you $1,000 for subcontract labor" creates accounting and 1099 confusion (see tax memo on W-9s and 1099-NEC).
- **Use the right agreement for each new job.** When you bring Ben or John onto a CWDB-built job, paper it under the subcontractor agreement, not the lead-purchase agreement.
- The amendment confirms in writing that subcontracted work is governed by a **separate** subcontractor agreement and not by the lead-purchase agreement.

## 4. LEAD-ECONOMICS DISCIPLINE WHEN CWDB BUILDS A SELF-GENERATED LEAD

When a lead CWDB generated is built by **CWDB itself** (not sold to a contractor), handle the internal accounting honestly:

- **No external accepted-bid fee is owed**, because no contractor bought that lead. Do not record a $1,000 lead fee receivable against a contractor for a job you built in-house.
- **Document the lead as built in-house.** In HubSpot / your books, mark the lead's disposition as a CWDB build, not as an external accepted bid. This keeps your funnel metrics honest and avoids any appearance that you are inflating contractor-side numbers.
- **Attribute the marketing cost to the construction line.** The ad spend that generated that lead is a cost of the construction line for that job, which supports clean per-line P&L (see tax memo, separate books).
- **If you want an internal "transfer price"** (charging the construction line a notional lead cost so each line's profitability is comparable), that is a fine management-accounting practice, but it is an **internal** entry only. It is not a real receivable, must not be reported to any contractor, and should be documented as an internal allocation. Confirm the approach with your CPA.

The principle: honest, documented accounting. The danger is blurring "CWDB built this" with "a contractor owes a fee," which would misstate both revenue and the funnel.

## 5. HONEST HOMEOWNER DISCLOSURE (FTC AND WIS. STAT. CH. 100)

When CWDB is the **builder**, the homeowner must understand they are contracting with **Central Wisconsin Deck Builders, LLC** to build their deck, not merely using a referral service. Blurring the lead-gen marketing with the build relationship in a way that misleads the homeowner creates exposure under:

- **FTC** prohibitions on deceptive acts or practices (Section 5 of the FTC Act), and
- **Wisconsin's deceptive trade practices law, Wis. Stat. ch. 100** (including Wis. Stat. ch. 100.18, which prohibits untrue, deceptive, or misleading representations to the public).

Practical rules:

- **The build contract names CWDB as the builder.** When CWDB performs the work, the homeowner signs a construction contract with **Central Wisconsin Deck Builders, LLC**, clearly identifying CWDB as the contractor responsible for the deck.
- **Do not imply neutral matchmaking when you are the seller of the service.** If marketing says "we connect you with trusted local builders," do not then quietly route the homeowner to your own crew while leaving them thinking they chose an independent third party. If CWDB is building it, say so.
- **Do not misuse "licensed and insured" or testimonial claims.** (Note: there is a separate open compliance item that the site testimonials are AI-generated, which is a distinct HIGH-risk issue handled elsewhere. Do not let build-side marketing repeat that problem.)
- **Honor Wisconsin home-improvement consumer protections.** Wisconsin's home improvement trade practices rules (ATCP 110) impose disclosure and contract-content requirements on home improvement sellers dealing directly with consumers. Now that CWDB contracts directly with homeowners for construction, **ATCP 110 likely applies to CWDB's build contracts.** Confirm the required contract disclosures with a Wisconsin attorney before signing homeowners to build jobs.

## 6. CONFIDENTIALITY: THE RISK RUNS THE OTHER WAY

A common worry is "can I use my own pricing data and estimator for my own builds?" **Yes.** CWDB owns its pricing database, its estimator tool, and its lead data. Using your own confidential business assets for your own construction jobs is entirely proper and is not a conflict.

The real confidentiality concern is the **reverse**: a subcontractor (Ben, John, or others) gaining access to CWDB's pricing models, estimator logic, lead sources, or homeowner data through a CWDB build, and then **misusing** it to compete, to poach homeowners, or to undercut CWDB. That risk is addressed by the **subcontractor agreement's confidentiality and data-handling terms** (mirroring Sections 8 and 9 of the lead-purchase agreement), which should:

- Restrict the sub's use of CWDB pricing, estimator output, and systems to the specific CWDB job.
- Prohibit the sub from soliciting the homeowner for future work that bypasses CWDB.
- Prohibit the sub from using CWDB confidential information to compete.
- Survive termination.

Make sure every sub working a CWDB job is under that subcontractor agreement **before** you share homeowner PII, pricing, or estimator detail with them.

## 7. DO / DON'T QUICK LIST

**DO:**

- Build your own jobs. Nothing in the contracts stops you.
- Use the **lead-purchase agreement** when a contractor buys a lead, and the **subcontractor agreement** when a contractor works for you.
- Keep the two relationships and their money flows separate, with separate paperwork.
- Document when a self-generated lead is built in-house, and account for the ad cost on the construction line.
- Name CWDB as the builder in homeowner construction contracts; comply with ATCP 110 disclosures.
- Use your own pricing and estimator freely for your own builds.

**DON'T:**

- Don't tell contractors one thing about lead access and then quietly undercut it with your in-house crew (good-faith covenant).
- Don't record an internal in-house build as an external contractor accepted-bid fee.
- Don't net lead fees against subcontract payments without explicit written agreement and CPA/attorney review.
- Don't mislead a homeowner into thinking they hired an independent third party when CWDB is the builder (FTC; Wis. Stat. ch. 100; ch. 100.18).
- Don't share CWDB pricing, estimator detail, or homeowner PII with a sub who has not signed the subcontractor agreement.
