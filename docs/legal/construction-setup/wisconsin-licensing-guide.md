## 1. The Two Wisconsin DSPS Credentials You Need

Wisconsin regulates residential construction on one- and two-family dwellings under Wis. Stat. ch. 101 (the safety and buildings statute that authorizes the Uniform Dwelling Code) and the credentialing rules administered by DSPS. Two credentials are central.

### 1.1 Dwelling Contractor Certification (the ENTITY credential)

- **Who holds it:** The business entity. Here, Central Wisconsin Deck Builders, LLC.
- **What it does:** This is the credential that a Wisconsin building department generally requires before it will issue a building permit for new construction or alteration of a one- or two-family dwelling. Without it, the LLC cannot pull residential permits in its own name.
- **Core requirement:** Proof of **commercial general liability insurance of at least $250,000 per occurrence** (Wis. Stat. 101.654(2); CWDB's spec targets $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate, which clears the floor) with a 30-day cancellation-notice-to-DSPS provision, plus proof of **workers' compensation insurance** OR a **valid workers' compensation exemption** (a solo owner with no employees typically qualifies; confirm the attestation in LicensE). See the companion insurance spec sheet for the full coverage spec.
- **Qualifier link:** The certified business must engage, as an employee, at least one **Dwelling Contractor Qualifier** (Section 1.2). The Certification is not complete or usable without a linked Qualifier.
- **Authority:** Wis. Stat. 101.654 and Wis. Admin. Code SPS 305.31. Application is **Form 3096** on the DSPS LicensE portal (license.wi.gov); credential term is 1 year.

### 1.2 Dwelling Contractor Qualifier (the INDIVIDUAL credential)

- **Who holds it:** A natural person. Jim should be CWDB's Qualifier.
- **What it does:** Demonstrates that a responsible individual associated with the business understands the Uniform Dwelling Code. The Qualifier is the human accountability anchor behind the entity Certification.
- **Core requirement:** Completion of an **approved 12-hour initial qualifier course** in dwelling construction (laws, codes, and business practices; the course carries its own tests, so there is no separate state exam), completed within one year before applying, plus **Form 3097** via LicensE. Credential term is 2 years. DSPS publishes an approved-provider list; see the operational companion for three concrete ~$99 online options.
- **One Qualifier per certified entity:** At least one Qualifier must be associated with the certified LLC. If Jim is the only field principal, Jim is the Qualifier.

### 1.3 Why You Need BOTH and in What Order

- **Jim personally** needs the **Qualifier** (the 12-hour course and application).
- **CWDB the LLC** needs the **Certification** (entity credential, backed by insurance and the Qualifier link).
- You cannot finalize the entity **Certification** without (a) insurance in place and (b) a linked **Qualifier**. So the order is insurance, then Qualifier, then Certification, then permits.

**Risk if skipped: HIGH.** Pulling or attempting to pull a residential permit without a valid Dwelling Contractor Certification can trigger permit denial, enforcement, and potential penalties, and can undermine the enforceability of your homeowner contract.

## 3. Local Requirements: City of Wausau and Marathon County

Wisconsin's UDC is enforced locally, so the building permit itself comes from the municipal or county building department, not the state.

### 3.1 Where to Pull Permits

- **Inside City of Wausau limits:** apply through the **City of Wausau** building/inspection department.
- **Unincorporated Marathon County and many surrounding towns:** apply through the **Marathon County** building inspection program, or the town's designated inspection agency. Schofield, Weston, Mosinee, and Merrill each have their own jurisdictional handling; confirm per-municipality which office issues the deck permit. (Verify the current intake office and online portal with each local building department.)

### 3.2 Local Contractor Registration

Some Wisconsin municipalities require a **local contractor registration or license** in addition to the state Dwelling Contractor Certification before they will issue permits to your business. **Verify with the City of Wausau and Marathon County** whether a separate local registration is required and what the fee and renewal cadence are. Treat this as a checklist item per jurisdiction, not an assumption.

### 3.3 What Triggers a Deck Permit

A building permit is generally required for a new deck or a structural alteration to an existing deck. Minor cosmetic work (for example, re-staining an existing structurally sound deck) typically does not by itself require a building permit, but **verify the local threshold** (some jurisdictions exempt very small or ground-level platforms below a set height or area). When in doubt, call the building department before starting.

### 3.4 What Gets Inspected

Inspectors focus on the structural items that fail most often on decks:

- **Ledger attachment** to the house (proper fasteners, flashing, and connection to the band joist, not just nailed siding).
- **Footings** (depth below frost line, size, and bearing).
- **Guardrail height** and baluster spacing.
- **Load capacity** (joist sizing, spacing, span, and beam sizing for the design load).

Build to the prescriptive standards so inspections pass on the first visit.

## 4. Wisconsin Uniform Dwelling Code: Deck Provisions

The UDC governs one- and two-family dwellings and is published in the Wisconsin Administrative Code SPS chapters, principally:

- **SPS 320** (administration and scope of the UDC).
- **SPS 321** (construction standards, including the structural provisions that govern decks: footings, ledger attachment, guards/guardrails, joist and beam sizing, and live/dead load).
- **SPS 322** (energy: not typically deck-relevant but part of the same dwelling-code family).
- **SPS 323** (heating, ventilating, and air conditioning: not deck-relevant).
- **SPS 325** (electrical: relevant only if the deck includes lighting, receptacles, or other wiring).

Wisconsin has adopted **deck-specific prescriptive standards** within the UDC framework so builders can size members, footings, and connections from tables rather than engineering each deck. Practical takeaways:

- **Permit trigger:** new deck or structural alteration (Section 3.3).
- **Inspected items:** ledger, footings, guardrail height and spacing, and load (Section 3.4).
- **Build to the prescriptive tables** in SPS 321 for joist span, beam span, post sizing, footing size and depth, ledger fastening, and guard requirements. **Verify the current edition/code-cycle tables** with the local building department, because Wisconsin periodically updates adopted code editions.

**Risk note: MEDIUM.** The most common deck failures and inspection rejections are ledger attachment and footing depth. Get these right on every job to protect both occupant safety and CWDB's liability exposure.

## 5. EPA Renovation, Repair and Painting (RRP) Lead-Paint Rule

This matters specifically because CWDB is adding **staining and refinishing** work.

### 5.1 When RRP Applies

The EPA RRP Rule (40 CFR Part 745, enforced in Wisconsin) applies to renovation, repair, and painting activities that **disturb painted surfaces in housing built before 1978** (and child-occupied facilities). For a deck builder/stainer, the trigger is realistic when you **sand, scrape, power-wash aggressively, or otherwise disturb old painted or coated surfaces** on a pre-1978 home, for example refinishing an attached deck, railing, porch, or any work that disturbs paint on the house structure itself. New construction on a post-1978 home generally does not trigger RRP.

### 5.2 Certification Required When Triggered

- **Firm certification:** the business (CWDB) must be an EPA-certified renovation **firm**.
- **Individual certification:** at least one **certified renovator** must be trained and oversee the work, with the renovator having completed an accredited RRP course.
- (Verify current firm and renovator certification fees, course length, and renewal cadence with EPA/WI DSPS, since these are periodically updated. Firm certification has historically run on a multi-year renewal cycle.)

### 5.3 Exposure Rating and How to Avoid It

**Exposure rating: MEDIUM**, and easily managed. To avoid unnecessary RRP cost and risk:

- **Screen the home's age.** If built 1978 or later, RRP generally does not apply.
- **For pre-1978 homes, test the surface** to be disturbed for lead-based paint using an EPA-recognized test or kit. If no lead is present and documented, RRP requirements generally do not attach to that surface.
- **If lead is present or untested on pre-1978 housing, use a certified renovator and certified firm** and follow lead-safe work practices.
- **Build the age screen into your intake** so the question is answered before a stainer ever touches the surface.
