## Executive Summary (Plain English)

CWDB is one LLC that now runs **two different businesses**: a lead-generation business and a hands-on **deck construction and staining** business. This single fact drives the most important instruction on this sheet: **tell the carrier about both operations.** If the carrier underwrites CWDB as a marketing/lead-gen company and later learns it was building decks, a construction claim can be denied and coverage effectively voided.

The coverage CWDB needs as a deck builder/stainer, in priority order:

## 1. Commercial General Liability (CGL)

- **Recommended limits:** $1,000,000 per occurrence / $2,000,000 general aggregate. Consider a per-project aggregate endorsement so one job's claims do not erode the limit available to other jobs.
- **Classification:** Must be classified for **CONSTRUCTION operations** (deck building and exterior wood staining/refinishing), not just office/marketing. Ask the broker to confirm the construction class code(s) on the policy.
- **What it protects:** Third-party bodily injury and property damage arising from your work, for example a guest injured by a deck collapse, damage to the home during installation, or a slip caused by jobsite conditions. This is the core liability shield for a builder.
- **Why it matters for CWDB:** The Wisconsin DSPS Dwelling Contractor Certification requires proof of commercial general liability insurance, so CGL is both a business protection and a licensing prerequisite. See the companion Wisconsin licensing guide.

### 1.1 CRITICAL: Dual-Operations Disclosure (Do Not Skip)

CWDB is **one LLC that runs both a lead-generation business and a construction business.** The carrier MUST be told, in writing, that the same insured entity:

- generates and sells homeowner project leads (lead generation / marketing), AND
- performs hands-on deck construction and staining.

**Why this is non-negotiable:** If the policy is underwritten only for marketing/lead-gen and a construction loss occurs, the carrier can deny the claim and treat coverage as void for misrepresentation of operations. Disclosing both operations up front gets the construction exposure properly classified and rated so claims actually pay.

**Risk if omitted: HIGH.** Instruct the broker on the application that the named insured conducts dual operations (lead generation AND residential deck construction/staining) and request written confirmation that both are covered.

## 2. Commercial Auto

- **Recommended minimum:** **Hired and non-owned auto liability** (HNOA) at minimum, covering personal or rented vehicles used for business errands and jobsite travel.
- **If a work truck is used for the business:** add **owned commercial auto** liability and physical damage. A personal auto policy generally will not cover an accident while the vehicle is being used for the construction business.
- **What it protects:** Bodily injury and property damage from vehicle use in the course of the business, including hauling materials and tools to jobsites.
- **Why it matters:** A deck builder is constantly moving lumber, tools, and crew. Without commercial auto, an at-fault accident on a material run can be uncovered.

---

## 3. Inland Marine (Tools and Equipment Floater)

- **Recommended:** A contractors **tools-and-equipment floater** sized to the replacement value of your tools (verify total tool value with the broker to set the limit).
- **What it protects:** Theft, loss, or damage to portable tools and equipment on the jobsite, in transit, or in storage. Standard property and auto policies usually will not cover tools stolen from a truck or jobsite.
- **Why it matters:** Tool theft from trucks and open jobsites is common. This floater keeps a single theft from stalling production.

---

## 4. Installation Floater / Builders Risk

- **Recommended:** An **installation floater** (often the right fit for a deck builder) or **builders risk** covering materials and work in progress at the jobsite until the project is complete and accepted.
- **What it protects:** Loss or damage to your **materials and partially completed work** before handoff, for example lumber and a half-built deck damaged by fire, wind, or theft on site.
- **Why it matters:** Until the deck is finished and accepted, the materials and labor invested are at risk and are generally not covered by the homeowner's policy or by CGL.

---

## 5. Workers' Compensation

- **Solo owner with no employees:** Workers' compensation is **generally NOT required** for a single-member owner-operator with no employees. (Verify the current solo-owner treatment and any exemption filing with the broker and WI DSPS.)
- **When it becomes mandatory:** Wisconsin requires workers' compensation once the business has **employees** meeting the statutory threshold (historically tied to having employees or reaching a quarterly payroll trigger). **Verify the current employee/payroll threshold** under Wis. Stat. ch. 102 before hiring anyone, including the first part-time helper.
- **The legal backstop for subcontractors:** Under **Wis. Stat. ch. 102**, a prime/general contractor can be held liable for the **workers' compensation obligations of an uninsured subcontractor's employees.** In plain terms: if Jim acts as the prime contractor and hires a sub whose own workers' comp has lapsed, an injury to that sub's worker can land on CWDB.
- **What it protects:** Medical costs and lost wages for work-related injuries to employees (and, via the backstop, exposure created by uninsured subs).

**Risk rating: MEDIUM to HIGH.** Low while Jim is truly solo with no subs. It climbs to HIGH the moment Ben Barton (Barton Builders LLC) or John Garcia (John Garcia Construction, LLC) convert from partners to **subcontractors**, because their workers' comp status becomes CWDB's exposure. Mitigate by (a) carrying or obtaining workers' comp as soon as you have employees, and (b) collecting current workers' comp certificates or valid exemptions from every sub before work starts (Section 8).

## 6. Umbrella / Excess Liability

- **Recommended:** An **umbrella/excess** layer (commonly starting at $1,000,000) sitting on top of CGL, commercial auto, and (where applicable) employers liability.
- **What it protects:** Catastrophic claims that exceed the underlying policy limits, for example a serious injury from a deck failure where damages run past the $1M CGL per-occurrence limit.
- **Why it matters:** Deck failures can cause severe injuries to multiple people at once (a crowded deck collapse). The umbrella is the affordable layer that prevents a single large claim from threatening the business and Jim's interests.

## 8. Certificates of Insurance You Must COLLECT From Every Subcontractor

This mirrors what CWDB already requires of its contractor partners. **Before any subcontractor sets foot on a jobsite,** collect and keep on file:

- [ ] **Current Commercial General Liability** certificate with adequate limits (recommend the sub carry at least the same $1M / $2M as CWDB; verify minimums with the broker).
- [ ] **Workers' Compensation** certificate **or a valid Wisconsin workers' comp exemption** for the sub. This is the direct defense against the Wis. Stat. ch. 102 prime-contractor backstop in Section 5.
- [ ] **Additional-insured status for CWDB** named on the sub's CGL policy (request an additional-insured endorsement, not just a mention on the certificate).
- [ ] **Minimum-limits met before work starts.** No sub begins work until the COI is in hand, verified current, and meets the required minimums.

**Operational rule:** No COI on file means no work on site. Set a calendar reminder to re-collect COIs at each policy expiration so a lapsed sub policy never goes unnoticed.

This directly applies to **Ben Barton (Barton Builders LLC)** and **John Garcia (John Garcia Construction, LLC)** once they convert from partners to subcontractors as CWDB's volume grows.
