## 1. WISCONSIN SALES AND USE TAX ON REAL-PROPERTY CONSTRUCTION

### 1.1 The general rule: the contractor is the consumer

Wisconsin sales and use tax is imposed under **Wis. Stat. ch. 77** (subchapter III, the sales and use tax). For **real-property construction activities**, Wisconsin follows a long-standing framework, reflected in DOR real-property-construction guidance (historically DOR Publication 207, Sales and Use Tax Information for Contractors), under which a contractor who builds or improves real property is treated as the **consumer** of the materials it installs.

The practical consequences of the consumer rule:

- **You pay sales or use tax when you buy materials.** When you purchase lumber, joist hangers, fasteners, concrete for footings, railing, decking boards, stain, and similar items that become a permanent part of the homeowner's deck, you pay Wisconsin sales tax at the register (or accrue **use tax** under Wis. Stat. ch. 77 if you buy from an out-of-state or untaxed source). You do **not** buy those materials tax-free for resale, because you are not reselling them as tangible personal property; you are consuming them in improving real property.
- **You generally do not charge the homeowner sales tax on the improvement.** A charge to the homeowner for constructing or attaching a deck to their home (an improvement to real property) is generally **not a taxable retail sale**. You bill the homeowner a lump sum (or labor-plus-materials) for the improvement, and that charge is generally not subject to sales tax collected from the homeowner.

This is why a real-property contractor's invoice to a homeowner usually has **no separate sales-tax line**: the tax was already paid upstream when the contractor bought the materials.

### 1.2 What this means operationally for CWDB

- Do **not** add a sales-tax line to a homeowner's deck-build invoice for a real-property improvement, unless the DOR or your CPA tells you a particular charge is taxable.
- **Do** budget the sales/use tax you pay on materials as a **cost of goods**, because you are the one who bears it.
- If you ever sell tangible personal property that is **not** installed as a real-property improvement (for example, selling a homeowner a freestanding planter box, a portable item, or loose materials they install themselves), that can be a **taxable retail sale** where you must collect sales tax. That is a different transaction type and would require a **seller's permit**. Flag any such sales to your CPA.

> Confirm with the Wisconsin Department of Revenue and a CPA. The contractor-as-consumer treatment is the standard Wisconsin framework, but the correct handling of any specific charge depends on the facts and on current DOR guidance.

## 2. THE OPEN QUESTION: STAINING AND REFINISHING AN EXISTING DECK

New deck construction is clearly a **real-property improvement**, so the contractor-as-consumer rule in Section 1 applies cleanly. **Re-staining or refinishing a deck that already exists is the gray area** and must be confirmed with the WI DOR.

The uncertainty exists because Wisconsin distinguishes between:

- **Real-property improvements** (generally nontaxable to the customer; contractor pays tax on materials), versus
- **Repair, service, maintenance, or processing of tangible personal property**, and certain enumerated taxable services, which can be **taxable** to the customer under Wis. Stat. ch. 77.

### 2.1 Considerations that bear on the staining question

- **Is the deck real property at the time of the work?** A deck permanently attached to a home is typically part of the real property. Work on real property leans toward the improvement (nontaxable-to-customer) side, but staining is closer to maintenance than to new construction, which is exactly why a determination is warranted.
- **Improvement versus maintenance/repair characterization.** Building or replacing a deck adds or restores significant value and is an improvement. Periodic re-staining to protect or refresh an existing surface can look more like **maintenance or servicing**, which the DOR may treat differently.
- **Bundled versus standalone.** Staining performed as part of a single deck-build contract may follow the treatment of the overall improvement. **Standalone** re-staining of an existing deck (no construction) is the transaction most likely to be characterized differently, so price and document those jobs carefully until you have a DOR answer.
- **Materials versus labor split.** How you itemize (lump-sum improvement versus separately stated labor and materials) can affect treatment. Until confirmed, avoid assuming a standalone staining job is automatically nontaxable.

### 2.2 Recommended action on staining

Before you price or sell **standalone** deck staining/refinishing jobs, obtain DOR guidance (a written determination request to the DOR is the cleanest path) on whether standalone re-staining of an existing attached deck is (a) a nontaxable real-property improvement, or (b) a taxable repair/service. Until you have that answer, treat standalone staining as **potentially taxable** and have your CPA hold any collected tax appropriately so you are not under-collected if the DOR says it is taxable.

> Confirm with the Wisconsin Department of Revenue and a CPA. Do not treat the staining characterization as settled.

## 3. INCOME TAX: HOW CONSTRUCTION REVENUE IS TREATED INSIDE THE LLC

### 3.1 Default: disregarded entity, Schedule C

Central Wisconsin Deck Builders, LLC is a **Wisconsin single-member LLC** organized under **Wis. Stat. ch. 183**. For federal income tax, a single-member LLC is by default a **disregarded entity** (IRS entity-classification "check-the-box" rules). That means:

- The LLC itself does not file a separate federal income tax return.
- All net income from **both** lines (lead-gen fees and construction profit) is reported on **your personal Form 1040**, on **Schedule C**.
- You may use **two Schedule C's**, one for the lead-generation line and one for the construction line, since they are distinct trades or businesses. This pairs naturally with the separate-books requirement in Section 5 and gives cleaner per-line profitability.
- Net Schedule C income is subject to **self-employment tax** (Social Security and Medicare) in addition to income tax.
- Wisconsin income tax follows similarly: the income flows through to your Wisconsin individual return.

### 3.2 The S-Corp election interplay (time-sensitive)

You can elect to have the LLC taxed as an **S corporation** (file Form 2553; Form 8832 may also be relevant depending on path). The trade-offs:

- **Potential self-employment-tax savings.** As an S-Corp, only your **reasonable salary** is subject to employment taxes; remaining profit distributed to you is generally not subject to self-employment tax. With two active revenue lines, profit could grow enough to make this worthwhile.
- **Reasonable-salary requirement.** The IRS requires an S-Corp owner-employee who works in the business to pay themselves a **reasonable salary** through real payroll (with withholding, payroll filings, and a W-2). You cannot zero out salary to avoid all employment tax.
- **Added administrative cost.** S-Corp status means payroll setup, a separate 1120-S return, and more bookkeeping. The savings must exceed those costs.
- **The window: about 2026-06-20.** Your S-Corp election timing window closes around June 20, 2026. If you want S-Corp treatment for the current tax year on the standard timeline, the election generally must be made within the window. Missing it usually means waiting or pursuing late-election relief.

**This is a CPA decision, and it is time-sensitive.** Get a CPA's recommendation now. The right answer depends on projected combined profit from lead-gen plus construction, the reasonable salary you would have to pay, and the administrative cost you are willing to carry.

> Confirm with a CPA. Do not let the election window lapse by default without a documented decision.

## 4. 1099-NEC ISSUANCE TO SUBCONTRACTORS

As construction volume grows, Ben Barton (Barton Builders LLC), John Garcia (John Garcia Construction, LLC), and others will perform work for the LLC as **subcontractors**. The LLC then becomes a payer with information-reporting obligations:

- **Collect a Form W-9 BEFORE you pay any subcontractor.** The W-9 gives you the payee's legal name, business name, entity type, and taxpayer identification number (EIN or SSN). Collect it before the first payment so you are not chasing it at year-end (and so you can apply backup withholding rules if a payee refuses).
- **Issue Form 1099-NEC** to each subcontractor you pay **$600 or more** during a calendar year for services, as required under the Internal Revenue Code information-reporting rules. 1099-NEC is generally **not** required for payments to a corporation, but you still collect the W-9 to document that and to confirm entity type.
- **Keep records** of each payment by payee (date, amount, project, method) so the year-end 1099 reconciles to your books.
- **Note the two relationships.** Ben and John are also lead **buyers** under the Contractor Lead Purchase Agreement, where **they pay the LLC** $1,000 per accepted bid (and the LLC issues **them** a 1099 only if the LLC is the payer; in that relationship the LLC is the payee, so the contractor may 1099 the LLC). Keep the two cash flows and their reporting separate. See the companion conflict-of-interest-and-channel memo and the contractor agreement amendment.

> Confirm 1099 thresholds and corporate-payee exceptions with a CPA each tax year, as IRS thresholds and forms can change.

## 5. SEPARATE BOOKS: TAX ACCURACY AND THE LIABILITY SHIELD

Keep the **lead-generation line** and the **construction line** on **separate books** within the LLC. This ties to the LLC operating agreement's recordkeeping expectations and to **Wis. Stat. ch. 183**, and it matters on two fronts:

1. **Tax accuracy.** Separate ledgers (or class/segment tracking inside one accounting system) let you produce a clean Schedule C per line, correctly characterize sales/use tax on construction materials, and substantiate deductions. This is essential if the staining sales-tax question (Section 2) ever requires you to show how a given job was treated.

2. **The liability shield.** The LLC's protection of your personal assets under Wis. Stat. ch. 183 depends in part on respecting the entity as separate from you personally. **Commingling** personal and business funds, or sloppy records that blur the entity, is a classic argument used to "pierce the veil." Adding a second, higher-risk activity (you now physically build and stain decks, which carries construction-defect and injury exposure the pure lead-gen line did not) raises the stakes. Clean, separated books, a dedicated business bank account, and documented inter-line accounting all reinforce the shield.

You do **not** necessarily need two bank accounts, but you **do** need internal accounting that cleanly separates the two lines. A single business operating account with disciplined class tracking is acceptable if your CPA agrees; two accounts can make separation even cleaner.

> Confirm the recordkeeping approach with your CPA, and review the operating agreement's books-and-records provisions.
